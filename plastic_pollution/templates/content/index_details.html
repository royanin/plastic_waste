Ocean plastic pollution data visualization


Here we look at some data and stories related to plastic pollution, and ocean plastic pollution in particular. When looking at the problem of plastic pollution in the ocean, it’s important to realize that while projects to clean up plastic already in the ocean or other waterways get a lot of media coverage, investment in waste management before the plastic gets in the ocean is more effective as well as being more cost effective. Reducing plastic consumption worldwide and investing in improved waste management infrastructure in lower-income countries hold promise for lessening plastic pollution in the ocean.



{# estimate 8 million MT #}

An estimated 8 million metric ton (MT) of plastic waste enter the ocean every year


How much is 8 million MT?

8 million MT of plastic, compacted, would fill about 1.2 million freight train cars. If they were all linked together traveling 30 mph (about 50 km/h), it would take 14 days for all 8 million MT of plastic to pass.

Plastic is not biodegradable, so once it’s in the ocean, it remains there.

Data:[EPA Australia Waste Density] and [Shipping container volume]



Reducing the amount of plastic already in the ocean


Once it enters waterways, plastic may be recaptured before it reaches the ocean. Two examples of automated devices which are already doing this are Mr. Trash Wheel, located in the inner harbor in Baltimore, MD, USA, and the SeaBin project, located in waterways worldwide. Once plastic has entered the open ocean, removal is more difficult because of strong currents and the vastness of the ocean, and the technology is still nascent in this area. Under development now is a device by The Ocean Cleanup built to remove plastic from the open ocean.

Waste management on land is cheaper



Approximate cost of removing 1 MT plastic waste from the ocean, near harbors, and via municipal waste collection on land.


In general, the cost of proper waste management on land is less than the cost of cleaning up plastic already in the ocean. Initial purchase and operation of Mr. Trash Wheel or an individual SeaBin corresponds to roughly USD $1,000 per ton of trash removed from the water. The Ocean Cleanup device is projected to cost around USD $5,000 per ton of plastic removed once it is operational. On the other hand, proper waste management to prevent plastic entering the waterways costs an average of USD $160/ton.

However, the waste management cost on land varies widely based on where we are in the world. In fact, it’s not just the waste management cost -- but our plastic waste generation and our ability to manage the waste are strongly related to the economic condition of a country. Below we explore some of these variations.

Countries are classified as high, middle, or low income



High, middle, and low income countries are defined by the world bank as GNI/capita over $12,000, between $1,000 and $12,000, and under $1,000 respectively. As countries become wealthier, their consumption of many things, including single-use plastic, increases. With growing consumption of plastic, the amount discarded also goes up.

{# well managed waste #}

How much plastic waste is managed well globally?


What is well managed waste?

Most plastic waste from high income countries is well managed, while only about 32% of plastic waste from middle income, and 20% from low income countries is managed well. While the highest plastic use per person occurs in high income countries, lower income countries have less developed waste management infrastructure and smaller budgets, leading to more plastic which can be discharged into waterways.

Data: [Jambeck 2015], [World Bank What a Waste Global Database]

What do we mean by well managed waste?

Plastic waste can be managed in three ways which effectively prevent its release into waterways and the environment in general--recycling, incineration, or landfilling. All three options require a large investment in infrastructure which is not always available, meaning not all plastic waste in all locations is contained. Poorly-managed waste can enter the world’s waterways, where it can enter the ocean or lead to environmental damage.

How much is spent on waste management?



Approximate % of city budgets spent on waste management in high, middle, and low income countries

The expansion of waste management infrastructure has not kept up with the expanded production of plastic waste, which may be explained by a combination of the very low cost of plastic and the relatively high cost of effective waste disposal. Cities in low and middle income countries spend a much higher proportion of their municipal budget on waste disposal compared to those in high income countries.

Data: [World Bank What a Waste 2.0 2018]

How far would $1,000 go to clean up plastic waste?



If an organization had $1,000 to spend to improve the problem of ocean plastic pollution, what would give the most benefit? While cleaning up plastic already in waterways is important, about 100 times as much plastic waste can be cleaned up at the same cost in a low-income country, or 50 times as much in a middle-income country.

Data: [Mr. Trash Wheel], [Seabin Project], [The Ocean Cleanup], [World Bank What a Waste 2.0 2018]



Take-home message


To stem ocean plastic pollution, it is significantly more cost-effective to invest in waste-management infrastructure, both conventional and innovative, in low- and middle-income countries. Building up waste management infrastructure, along with reduce and reuse, will also improve other environmental issues such as air and water pollution, thus ensuring a healthier planet for years (and generations) to come.
